The \texttt{heart-disease.csv} dataset contains 462 male patients from the
Western Cape, South Africa, described by nine clinical features and a binary
target \texttt{chd} (1\,=\,CHD present) with no missing values.
Four features exceed the $|\gamma_{1}|>1$ skewness threshold: \texttt{alcohol}
($\gamma_{1}=2.31$), \texttt{tobacco} ($\gamma_{1}=2.08$), \texttt{ldl}
($\gamma_{1}=1.31$), and \texttt{sbp} ($\gamma_{1}=1.18$), suggesting
log-transformation may improve classifier performance.
Table~\ref{tab:desc} summarises the key descriptive statistics. Four features

Of the 462 patients, 160 (34.6\%) are CHD-positive and 302 (65.4\%)
CHD-negative, giving an imbalance ratio of 1.89:1. This means raw accuracy
will be a misleading evaluation metric
\cite{rehman2025predicting, banerjee2025systematic, dutta2019efficient, borges2025logistic}
and alternative measures such as AUC and F1-score are used throughout.
The Pearson correlation heatmap (Figure~\ref{fig:corr}) reveals that
\texttt{age} ($r=0.37$) is the strongest linear predictor of CHD, followed
by \texttt{tobacco} ($r=0.30$), \texttt{famhist} ($r=0.27$), and \texttt{ldl}
($r=0.26$).

Violin plots stratified by CHD class confirm that \texttt{age}, \texttt{tobacco},
and \texttt{ldl} show the clearest class-conditional separation, while
\texttt{obesity} and \texttt{typea} distributions overlap substantially.

Log\,\textsubscript{1p}-transformation of the four dynamically identified
skewed features reduces their skewness markedly (Figure~\ref{fig:logtr}):
\texttt{alcohol} from 2.31 to 0.44, \texttt{tobacco} from 2.08 to 0.53,
\texttt{ldl} from 1.31 to 0.50, and \texttt{sbp} from 1.18 to 0.48.
These transformations are applied as a preprocessing option in the
modelling stages (Sections~\ref{sec:lrrp}--\ref{sec:oc}).

Feature importance was assessed jointly using mutual information (MI) and
the ANOVA F-test \cite{elsofany2024proposed, ullah2024machine}. Using two
complementary criteria guards against misleading rankings from linear
assumptions alone. Table~\ref{tab:fi} shows the normalised scores: \texttt{age}
ranks first under both criteria, while \texttt{obesity} and \texttt{typea}
score lowest. These rankings directly inform the variable selection strategy
for the ridge logistic regression in Section~\ref{sec:lrrp}.

PCA was applied to the standardised feature matrix
\cite{banerjee2025systematic}. PC1 explains 32.1\% of variance and loads
positively on \texttt{adiposity} (0.52), \texttt{age} (0.46), and
\texttt{obesity} (0.40) --- a latent \emph{metabolic risk} axis. PC2 is
driven by \texttt{alcohol} (0.54) and \texttt{tobacco} (0.46), reflecting a
\emph{lifestyle} axis. Seven components are needed to reach 90\% cumulative
variance, indicating moderate intrinsic dimensionality. The 2-D
projection (Figure~\ref{fig:pca}) shows partial but incomplete class
separation; the loadings heatmap (Figure~\ref{fig:pcaload}) confirms PC1
captures metabolic risk while PC2 captures lifestyle behaviours, motivating
non-linear classifiers in Section~\ref{sec:oc}.

\section{Logistic Regression with Ridge Penalty}
\label{sec:lrrp}
% =============================================================================

Logistic regression with an L2 (ridge) penalty was chosen as the primary
interpretable classifier. The multicollinearity between \texttt{adiposity}
and \texttt{obesity} ($r=0.72$, Section~\ref{sec:eda}) motivates
regularisation: ridge shrinks correlated coefficients jointly without zeroing
any out \cite{borges2025logistic, hassan2022effectively}.

\textbf{Feature engineering.}
Two multiplicative interaction terms were derived from the nine original
variables to capture joint effects that a linear model cannot represent
with base features alone.

\textit{(i) \texttt{age\_tobacco} $=$ age $\times$ tobacco.}
The \texttt{tobacco} column records cumulative lifetime consumption in kg,
not a daily rate. A young patient and an older patient may carry the same
lifetime total yet differ substantially in their cardiovascular trajectory:
the older patient has sustained arterial exposure for far longer.
Multiplying age by tobacco encodes both quantity and duration of exposure in
a single term. \textbf{Assumption:} the marginal CHD risk of additional
tobacco consumption is multiplicative with age, i.e.\ the dose--risk
relationship compounds over decades rather than remaining constant
\cite{rehman2025predicting}.

\textit{(ii) \texttt{age\_famhist} $=$ age $\times$ famhist.}
Family history is a binary genetic risk indicator. Genetic predisposition
is not static: a 60-year-old with family history represents a materially
higher immediate risk than a 30-year-old with the same flag. The
interaction allows the model to represent this compounding effect, which
ridge regularisation would otherwise suppress by shrinking the
\texttt{famhist} coefficient towards zero. \textbf{Assumption:} the
influence of family history amplifies with age rather than remaining
additive and age-independent. Because \texttt{famhist} is binary, the
term is equivalent to a piecewise age slope — positive for patients with
family history and zero otherwise.

\textbf{Limitations.} Both terms inherit the measurement limitations of
their parent variables (self-reported tobacco total; binary family history
without severity grading). The multiplicative form imposes a specific
functional shape; a flexible non-parametric model may better approximate
the true relationship.

\textbf{Preprocessing.}
\texttt{famhist} encoded as 0/1; skewed features (\texttt{sbp},
\texttt{tobacco}, \texttt{ldl}, \texttt{alcohol})
log\,\textsubscript{1p}-transformed; all eleven features standardised with
\texttt{StandardScaler} (zero mean, unit variance).
The regularisation strength $C$ (inverse of $\lambda$) was selected by
stratified 5-fold cross-validation over the grid
$\{0.001, 0.01, 0.1, 1, 10, 100\}$ maximising AUC-ROC on an 80\,\%
training set. Table~\ref{tab:coef} lists the standardised coefficients of
the best model ($C=0.01$).

The optimal $C=0.01$ implies strong regularisation, consistent with
multicollinear features reducing effective degrees of freedom.
\texttt{age} retains the largest effect ($\hat{\beta}=0.212$), now
followed by \texttt{age\_famhist} ($0.189$) and \texttt{ldl} ($0.174$),
indicating that the interaction of age with family history is a stronger
standalone signal than either raw predictor alone. The derived term
\texttt{age\_tobacco} ($0.125$) also outranks \texttt{typea}, \texttt{sbp},
and \texttt{adiposity}. \texttt{obesity} and \texttt{alcohol} remain
almost fully shrunk to zero.

On the held-out test set ($n=93$, 20\,\%) the model achieves
AUC\,=\,0.820, F1\,=\,0.610, and accuracy\,=\,0.753
(Table~\ref{tab:perf}). The confusion matrix (Figure~\ref{fig:cm}) reveals
that CHD recall is 0.563 versus No-CHD recall of 0.871, a consequence of the
1.89:1 class imbalance. The derived interaction features improve recall by
12.5 percentage points relative to the nine-feature baseline. AUC of 0.820
is substantially above the 0.5 random baseline and competitive with published
benchmarks on similar datasets \cite{rehman2025predicting, ogunpola2024machine}.

The PCA 2-D projection (Section~\ref{sec:eda}) showed only partial linear
class separation, motivating non-linear alternatives to ridge logistic
regression. Five classifiers from the module were evaluated: a
\textbf{Decision Tree} (interpretable baseline), \textbf{Random Forest}
(bagged ensemble), \textbf{SVM with RBF kernel} (margin-based, non-linear),
\textbf{KNN} (non-parametric, distance-based), and \textbf{Gradient
Boosting} (sequential ensemble). Preprocessing was identical to
Section~\ref{sec:lrrp}: \texttt{famhist} encoded, four skewed features
log\,\textsubscript{1p}-transformed, all features standardised.

All five were screened by stratified 5-fold cross-validation on AUC-ROC
(Table~\ref{tab:cv}), using the same 11-feature set (including
\texttt{age\_tobacco} and \texttt{age\_famhist}) as Section~\ref{sec:lrrp}.
Decision Tree performed worst (CV-AUC\,=\,0.618), confirming that a single
tree overfits without pruning. Random Forest achieved the highest CV-AUC
(0.718), marginally ahead of SVM (0.716) and Gradient Boosting (0.699).
Random Forest was therefore selected for hyperparameter tuning.

GridSearchCV with 5-fold CV optimised three hyperparameters simultaneously:
\texttt{n\_estimators} $\in\{100, 300, 500\}$,
\texttt{max\_depth} $\in\{3, 5, \text{None}\}$,
\texttt{max\_features} $\in\{\text{sqrt}, \text{log2}\}$.
The best configuration was \texttt{max\_depth=3}, \texttt{max\_features=sqrt},
\texttt{n\_estimators=500} (tuned CV-AUC\,=\,0.742). Shallow trees
(\texttt{max\_depth=3}) prevent overfitting on a dataset of 462 observations.

ridge coefficients \cite{rehman2025predicting, ogunpola2024machine}.

\subsection*{Local Setup}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item Clone the repository and enter the project directory.
    \item Create and activate a virtual environment:
          \texttt{python -m venv .venv} then \texttt{.venv\textbackslash Scripts\textbackslash activate} (Windows).
    \item Install dependencies: \texttt{pip install -r requirements.txt}
    \item Run the full pipeline: \texttt{python chd\_main.py}\\
          Outputs saved to \texttt{exploratory\_data\_analysis/eda\_output/}.
    \item Launch dashboard: \texttt{streamlit run app.py}
\end{enumerate}

\noindent Python~$\geq$\,3.10 required. Key packages: \texttt{pandas\,$\geq$\,2.0},
\texttt{scikit-learn\,$\geq$\,1.4}, \texttt{streamlit\,$\geq$\,1.35},
