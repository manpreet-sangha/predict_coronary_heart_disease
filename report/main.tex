The dataset (\texttt{heart-disease.csv}, nine features, one binary label \texttt{chd} 
(1\,=\,CHD present), no missing values) contains 462 male patients from the 
Western Cape, South Africa. Table~\ref{tab:desc} shows that four features are right-skewed
\cite{rimal2025comparative} i.e.,
\texttt{alcohol} ($\gamma_{1}=2.31$), \texttt{tobacco} ($\gamma_{1}=2.08$),
\texttt{ldl} ($\gamma_{1}=1.31$), and \texttt{sbp} ($\gamma_{1}=1.18$) all
exceed the $|\gamma_{1}|>1$ threshold, which suggests a log-transformation
\cite{feng2014log} could help the model.

Of the 462 patients, 160 (34.6\%) have CHD and 302 (65.4\%) do not, resulting in a
class imbalance ratio of 1.89:1. Thus, instead of just using accuracy, I use AUC and F1-score 
as the main evaluation metrics \cite{rehman2025predicting, banerjee2025systematic, dutta2019efficient, borges2025logistic}.
In the Pearson correlation heatmap (Figure~\ref{fig:corr}), the feature most linearly associated with CHD is \texttt{age}
($r=0.37$), followed by \texttt{tobacco} ($r=0.30$), \texttt{famhist} ($r=0.27$), and \texttt{ldl}
($r=0.26$).

On the other hand, \texttt{alcohol} ($r=0.06$) and \texttt{obesity} ($r=0.10$)
show little linear association with CHD. I also noticed a fairly strong
correlation between \texttt{adiposity} and \texttt{obesity} ($r=0.72$), which
means these two features carry overlapping information. Multicollinearity is one of the reasons 
to choose ridge regularisation in Section~\ref{sec:lrrp} \cite{hassan2022effectively, ogunpola2024machine}.

Figure~\ref{fig:logtr} shows the effect of applying a
log\textsubscript{1p}-transformation to the four skewed features.
In each case the skewness drops well below the $|\gamma_1|>1$ threshold:
\texttt{sbp} falls from 1.18 to 0.73, \texttt{tobacco} from 2.08 to 0.25,
\texttt{ldl} from 1.31 to 0.16, and \texttt{alcohol} from 2.31 to 0.03.
After transformation the distributions look much closer to a bell curve
(blue vs.\ red in Figure~\ref{fig:logtr}), which helps models that assume
roughly symmetric inputs e.g. logistic regression and the
generative classifiers (LDA, QDA, Gaussian NB) used in this report.

To clearly demonstrate feature importance, I ranked them using
mutual information (MI) and the ANOVA F-test \cite{elsofany2024proposed, ullah2024machine}. 
I used two measures above as a single metric could be biased.
Table~\ref{tab:fi} shows the normalised scores side by side.
\texttt{age} comes top under both methods, while \texttt{obesity} and
\texttt{typea} score lowest. These rankings influenced which features to
include in the ridge logistic regression model in Section~\ref{sec:lrrp}.

To determine the underlying structure, I ran PCA on the standardised features
\cite{banerjee2025systematic}. PC1 captures 32.1\%

\texttt{age} (0.46), and \texttt{obesity} (0.40). 
PC2 picks up lifestyle factors, driven by

and non-CHD patients, meaning a straight (appropriate reference here) line cannot cleanly separate

\section{Logistic Regression with Ridge Penalty}
\label{sec:lrrp}
% =============================================================================

Logistic regression with an L2 (ridge) penalty
\cite{borges2025logistic} is chosen as the primary classifier, as instructed. 
Ridge penalty is preferred over lasso \cite{hassan2022effectively}
because lasso can force some coefficients to exactly zero, effectively
dropping features from the model. Ridge, on the other hand, shrinks all
coefficients towards zero but never removes any completely. Ridge also handles correlated features well by shrinking
them together, which is important given the multicollinearity observed between
\texttt{adiposity} and \texttt{obesity} ($r=0.72$, Section~\ref{sec:eda}).

\textbf{Feature engineering.}
Two features were derived to capture joint effects that a linear model cannot represent
with base features alone.

\textit{(i) \texttt{age\_tobacco} $=$ age $\times$ tobacco.}
The \texttt{tobacco} column records cumulative lifetime consumption (not daily rate).
Multiplying age by tobacco encodes both quantity and duration of exposure in
a single feature. \textbf{Assumption:} the risk from smoking gets worse
with age i.e., an older patient with the same tobacco total as a younger
one has had longer exposure, so their CHD risk is higher
\cite{rehman2025predicting}.

\textit{(ii) \texttt{age\_famhist} $=$ age $\times$ famhist.}
the influence of family history amplifies with age
\cite{patel2023famhist}. Because
\texttt{famhist} is binary, this feature term is equivalent to a piecewise age
slope i.e., positive for patients with family history and zero otherwise.

\textbf{Preprocessing.}
\texttt{famhist} encoded as 0/1; skewed features (\texttt{sbp},
\texttt{tobacco}, \texttt{ldl}, \texttt{alcohol})
log\,\textsubscript{1p}-transformed all eleven features standardised with
\texttt{StandardScaler} (zero mean, unit variance) prior to fitting, which is 
mandatory for ridge so the penalty treats all coefficients on the same scale
\cite{hassan2022effectively}.
The regularisation strength $C$ (inverse of $\lambda$) was selected by
stratified 5-fold cross-validation over $\{0.001,0.01,0.1,1,10,100\}$
maximising AUC-ROC on an 80\,\% training set.

Two logistic regression models are fitted: 
(i)~An unpenalised model via maximum likelihood that yields 
table coefficient, standard error, $z$-statistic, and $p$-value 
consistent with the lecture formulation
(Equation~\ref{eq:logit}). (ii)~The ridge model ($C=0.01$)
used for prediction uses L2 penalty that biases the coefficients towards zero,
which stabilises estimates under multicollinearity.

\begin{equation}
\log\!\left(\frac{\Pr(Y\!=\!1\mid X)}{\Pr(Y\!=\!0\mid X)}\right)
= \beta_0 + \beta_1 X_1 + \cdots + \beta_p X_p
\label{eq:logit}
\end{equation}
The inference table (Table~\ref{tab:inference}) shows that \texttt{ldl}
($z=2.84$, $p=0.005$), \texttt{typea} ($z=2.82$, $p=0.005$), and
\texttt{age} ($z=2.17$, $p=0.030$) are statistically significant predictors
under Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE). \texttt{tobacco}, \texttt{famhist}, \texttt{obesity}, and
\texttt{alcohol} are not statistically significant individually, partly due to
multicollinearity inflating their standard errors. The optimal $C=0.01$ (Table~\ref{tab:coef})
implies strong regularisation: \texttt{age} retains the largest ridge
effect ($\hat{\beta}=0.212$), followed by \texttt{age\_famhist} ($0.189$)
and \texttt{ldl} ($0.174$). \texttt{obesity} and \texttt{alcohol} are
shrunk near zero, consistent with their non-significance in the MLE fit.

\section{Alternative Classifiers}
\label{sec:oc}
% =============================================================================
The PCA 2-D projection (Section~\ref{sec:eda}) showed only partial linear
class separation, motivating alternatives to ridge logistic regression.
Twelve classifiers from the module were evaluated, spanning discriminative
models, tree-based ensembles, and generative classifiers.
Preprocessing was identical to Section~\ref{sec:lrrp}: \texttt{famhist}
encoded, four skewed features log\,\textsubscript{1p}-transformed, all
features standardised.

All twelve were screened by stratified 5-fold cross-validation on AUC-ROC
(Table~\ref{tab:final}), using the same 11-feature set (including
\texttt{age\_tobacco} and \texttt{age\_famhist}) as Section~\ref{sec:lrrp}.
Gaussian NB achieved the highest CV-AUC (0.765), followed by LDA (0.751)
and QDA (0.744). Among tree-based models, Extra Trees (0.730) and Random
Forest (0.718) led. The best CV performer (Gaussian NB) was tuned via
GridSearchCV; the best \texttt{var\_smoothing} was $10^{-12}$
(tuned CV-AUC\,=\,0.765). On the test set, however, QDA achieved the
highest AUC (0.828) and F1 (0.694), demonstrating that class-specific
covariance modelling benefits this dataset after log-transformation.

\textbf{Clinical relevance of recall.}
In CHD prediction, sensitivity (recall $= \text{TP}/(\text{TP}+\text{FN})$)
is the most clinically important metric: missing a patient who truly has
heart disease (false negative) risks delayed treatment and potentially a
fatal cardiac event, whereas a false positive leads only to further
diagnostic testing. Ridge LR achieves the highest precision (0.667) but
its CHD recall is only 0.563 --- it misses 44\% of CHD patients, which is
clinically unacceptable for a screening tool. Gaussian NB flips this
trade-off: recall rises to 0.750 (catches 75\% of CHD cases) at the cost
of lower precision (0.571), producing more false alarms but fewer missed
diagnoses. For a medical screening context, this is arguably preferable.
QDA achieves the best recall (0.781), correctly identifying 78\% of CHD
patients while maintaining the highest overall F1 (0.694) and accuracy
(0.763), making it the most clinically appropriate classifier in this
evaluation.

\textbf{Stability across repeated splits.}
A single train/test split can yield optimistic or pessimistic estimates
depending on which patients fall in the test set. Figure~\ref{fig:repeated}
shows the distribution of test AUC-ROC across 30 repeated stratified
80/20 splits. Ridge LR has the highest median AUC (0.778), followed by
LDA (0.772) and Gaussian NB (0.764). QDA, despite achieving the best
single-split AUC (0.828), drops to fourth (median 0.749) with wider
spread, indicating greater sensitivity to the specific split. This
confirms that the generative classifiers and Ridge LR are the most
\emph{consistently} strong performers on this dataset.

\textbf{Methods considered but excluded.}
\textit{Factor Analysis} (FA) is an unsupervised latent-variable model
designed for structure discovery, not prediction; PCA already fulfils this
exploratory role in Section~\ref{sec:eda}.
\textit{Fisher's DA} (FDA) for a binary outcome reduces to a single linear
discriminant direction mathematically equivalent to LDA under equal-covariance
assumptions --- redundant given LDA is already included.

\textbf{Why the chosen methods are appropriate.}
The 12 classifiers in Table~\ref{tab:final} span discriminative models
(Ridge LR, SVM, KNN), tree ensembles (Random Forest, Extra Trees, Gradient
Boosting, AdaBoost, LightGBM, Bagging, Decision Tree), and generative
models (Gaussian NB, LDA, QDA). Log-transformation of skewed features
mitigates the normality concerns, allowing generative classifiers to compete
effectively. Ridge logistic regression addresses the multicollinearity
identified in EDA without zeroing any predictor
\cite{hassan2022effectively}. This breadth ensures the partial non-linear
class separation visible in the PCA projection (Figure~\ref{fig:pca})
is captured across fundamentally different modelling paradigms.
